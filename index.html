The early days

Ah, where to begin? Our master, Christophe Plantin, is a man of mystery.

I’ve worked in his shop for many years, and though I see him every day, there’s a part of him that feels like a closed book.

Hi i’m Jaques, one of his oldest employees, i’m what they call an open book.

Throughout my time at the printing press, i racked up a bunch of interesting stories, today i’ll tell some of those to you, but keep your mouth shut! Plantin doesn’t like it when i run my mouth...

Someone once told me he was born in Saint-Avertin, near Tours in France in 1520, others say it was 1514... anyway, what I do know is that when he was just 14, his father left him behind in Paris so he could study. His father sadly died before he could return to Plantin.

Plantin and his wife moved to Antwerp, probably because of his big ambitions, what can I say, it was the right move to do for him!

An older worker told me he was a bookbinder at first. But one night, while Plantin was pondering the streets of Antwerp, he got attacked!

The injury to his arm left him unable to work as a bookbinder. And so he had no choice but to go for a different careerpath, he became a bookbinder. I tend to believe this story, it’s just too crazy to make up!

The art of book printing

Well, before i can tell you the juicy stories, i’ll first give some information on what book printing actually is, don’t worry i’ll keep it short and easy to understand!

Setting the type

First things first, setting the type. It all begins with selecting tiny metal letters, each one carefully chosen and arranged in reverse order. Line by painstaking line, these letters are assembled into a block of text, ready to tell a story or share a piece of knowledge. The process demands incredible focus and patience, as a single mistake—a letter placed in the wrong spot or upside down—can ruin an entire page.

The printing press

Once the type is set, the next step is to bring it to life with ink. We begin by coating the surface of the type with ink, applied using soft leather pads called ink balls. These pads are expertly dabbed and rolled across the tiny metal letters. It’s a delicate process, too much ink, and the letters will smudge, too little, and the impression will be faint. The ink itself is thick and rich, with a distinct smell that fills the air.

For a long time they believed the leather part was made of dogskin. This was never confirmed so believe what you want!

Printing is about so much more than just technique. It’s not just setting type, inking plates, and pulling levers—it’s about bringing ideas to life. It’s about stories, knowledge, and the way words can connect us all.

In our shop, we print books in Latin, Greek, Hebrew, and even more languages. It’s fascinating how words from so many places come together here, on our presses.

Illustrations in the 16th century

We also use intricate woodblocks for illustrations. These are carved by master artisans, their designs mirroring the intricacy of the written word.

Sometimes, I watch them at work and marvel at their skill. A single woodblock can take days to carve, but the result?

A masterpiece on every page.

Shady stories

Now that you understand the basics of the job you deserve to know some secret stories!

Master Plantin has always been a man of ambition, but ambition has its risks. He printed books that others might call… controversial.
