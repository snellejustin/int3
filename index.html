The early days

Ah, where to begin? Our master, Christophe Plantin, is a man of mystery.

I’ve worked in his shop for many years, and though I see him every day, there’s a part of him that feels like a closed book.

Hi i’m Jaques, one of his oldest employees. Throughout my time at the printing press, i racked up a bunch of interesting stories, today i’ll tell some of those to you, but keep your mouth shut! Plantin doesn’t like it when i run my mouth...

Someone once told me he was born in Saint-Avertin, near Tours in France in 1520, others say it was 1514... anyway, what I do know is that when he was just 14, his father left him behind in Paris so he could study. His father sadly died before he could return to Plantin.

Plantin and his wife moved to Antwerp, probably because of his big ambitions, what can I say, it was the right move to do for him!

An older worker told me he was a bookbinder at first. But one night, while Plantin was pondering the streets of Antwerp, he got attacked!

The injury to his arm left him unable to work as a bookbinder. And so he had no choice but to go for a different careerpath, he became a bookbinder. I tend to believe this story, it’s just too crazy to make up!

The art of book printing

Well, before i can tell you the juicy stories, i’ll first give some information on what book printing actually is, don’t worry i’ll keep it short and easy to understand!

Setting the type

First things first, setting the type. It all begins with selecting tiny metal letters, each one carefully chosen and arranged in reverse order. Line by painstaking line, these letters are assembled into a block of text, ready to tell a story or share a piece of knowledge. The process demands incredible focus and patience, as a single mistake—a letter placed in the wrong spot or upside down—can ruin an entire page.

The printing press

Once the type is set, the next step is to bring it to life with ink. We begin by coating the surface of the type with ink, applied using soft leather pads called ink balls. These pads are expertly dabbed and rolled across the tiny metal letters. It’s a delicate process, too much ink, and the letters will smudge, too little, and the impression will be faint. The ink itself is thick and rich, with a distinct smell that fills the air.

For a long time they believed the leather part was made of dogskin. This was never confirmed so believe what you want!

Pull the lever

Are you ready to print the next page? Use all your force and pull down the lever!

This handle is part of the press. It is used to imprint the letters on te paper. This is really heavy, only my strongest colleagues can work the lever a full day!

Printing is about so much more than just technique. It’s not just setting type, inking plates, and pulling levers—it’s about bringing ideas to life. It’s about stories, knowledge, and the way words can connect us all.

In our shop, we print books in Latin, Greek, Hebrew, and even more languages. It’s fascinating how words from so many places come together here, on our presses.

Illustrations in the 16th century

We also use intricate woodblocks for illustrations. These are carved by master artisans, their designs mirroring the intricacy of the written word.

Sometimes, I watch them at work and marvel at their skill. A single woodblock can take days to carve, but the result?

A masterpiece on every page.

psst... I know where we store 14.000 more of these wooden blocks, visit the museum in Antwerp to check them out in real life!

Shady stories

Now that you understand the basics of the job you deserve to know some secret stories!

Master Plantin has always been a man of ambition, but ambition has its risks. He printed books that others might call… controversial.

There even were times he printed Illegal books, after all, he was a business man doing business.

Printing illegal books

Man oh man, printing those illegal books isn’t all that easy, there was a lot of things to take into account while printing them. Who do you trust? Where and when do we print them? Of course everyone would do it in a different way, some probably won’t even take the risk. But my boss? he is just looking to do business, so you could say taking a calculated risk is his hobby.

Would you take the same approach as Plantin? Interact with following letters to find out!

Dear Jaques,

You raise an important question, and I agree it is prudent to tread carefully. Given the sensitive nature of these books, we shall use a pseudonym. My name is too closely tied to this shop and our other works, and it is not wise to draw undue attention to myself or our operations here.

Select a name that feels credible, yet distant enough from our circles to avoid suspicion. Ensure the typeface and imprint carry no traceable mark back to us. If inquiries arise, they must lead to dead ends. The protection of our craft and reputation is paramount, and this approach will shield us from unnecessary risks.

Proceed with the utmost care, and keep me informed of your progress.

Yours sincerely,
Christophe Plantin

Dear Jaques,

You were right to voice your concerns, and I must now share with you news that confirms the very fears you warned me about. Word has reached me that the Spanish authorities have seized a shipment of our books, a staggering 1000 copies. These were not printed here, as it happens, but by three of our French colleagues while I was away in Paris visiting an old friend.

Their actions were bold, perhaps too bold, and now the consequences have come down like a hammer. The Spanish have uncovered their operation, and the punishment was swift: forced labor aboard the galleys. A grim fate for anyone, but as fortune would have it, they managed to escape and have now fled back to France.

This news is both troubling and bewildering. Their escape may have spared them, but it leaves us in an uneasy position.

Yours sincerely,
Christophe Plantin

Friends at auction

He always has some tricks to outsmart everyone, when the company went bankrupt because of creditors, his friends bought the company at the public auction. This way we could continue to work for Plantin, as sneaky as it is, i must admire him for these moves!

Everything for freedom

One of my personal favourite stories is the one where he, and some other rich guys, tried to buy freedom of religion for 3 million gulden.

We printed the text of this petition at our printing shop. Spanish king Filip II denied, the inventors of the idea then used the money to buy troops against Filip, the inventors got caught and fled to Germany.

But my boss is a smart man, so instead of turning his back at the Spanish, he created a construction via the secretary of Filip II to get an order to make a new polyglotbible, named the Biblia Regia.

This way he could establish a good connection with the Spanish king, but most importantly wipe his sheet clean.

This the Biblia Regia, 1 of 4 different polyglotbibles. This book has 4907 pages and is written in 8 languages, all in the same book!
